ShinyHunters Exploits Oracle PeopleSoft Zero-Day (CVE-2026-35273) Across 100+ Universities
🔍 What happened: The ShinyHunters cybercrime group weaponized an unauthenticated remote code execution zero-day (CVE-2026-35273, CVSS 9.8) in Oracle PeopleSoft to breach more than 100 organizations between May 27 and June 9, stealing student records before Oracle published an advisory on June 10.
👤 Who's affected: Primarily higher-education institutions worldwide (68% of victims), with the University of Nottingham publicly confirming a breach exposing 454,600 current and former students' personal and academic data.
✅ What to do: Apply Oracle's out-of-band patch for PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools 8.61/8.62 immediately, audit for the README-IF-YOU-SEE-THIS-YOUVE-BEEN-HACKED.TXT marker file in PeopleSoft directories, and treat any administrative credentials as compromised.
▶ Full analysis & beginner explanation

CVE-2026-35273 resides in the Environment Management component of Oracle PeopleSoft Enterprise PeopleTools. Rated CVSS 9.8 out of 10, the flaw requires no authentication and no user interaction — an attacker only needs HTTP network access to execute arbitrary code as the server. ShinyHunters turned this bug into a large-scale extortion campaign, exfiltrating sensitive records and threatening to publish unless victims paid a ransom.

The attack spanned May 27 to June 9 across more than 300 vulnerable instances at over 100 organizations, and Oracle published its emergency advisory on June 10 — meaning the flaw was an active zero-day throughout the entire campaign window. Post-exploitation scripts were observed spreading laterally over SSH by brute-forcing internal hosts listed in /etc/hosts, then dropping a visible ransom marker file in PeopleSoft directories.

Data confirmed published on ShinyHunters' leak site already includes tens of gigabytes of student records: names, home addresses, phone numbers, emails, dates of birth, ethnicity, enrollment status, GPAs, majors, and student IDs. The University of Nottingham is the highest-profile confirmed victim with 454,600 records exposed. Mandiant notified over 100 organizations as victims.

🎓 For beginners: Oracle PeopleSoft is software used by universities to manage student records, HR, and finances. A "zero-day" means hackers found and exploited the bug before the company knew it existed and could release a fix. In this case, hundreds of thousands of students' personal information was stolen because a flaw let criminals walk right into school systems without even needing a password.

👤 Who's affected: Enterprise and government organizations globally relying on Fortinet FortiGate for perimeter defense and VPN access, with compromised credentials spanning 21,600+ domains including credentials, session tokens, and authentication hashes for 24 protocols.
✅ What to do: Immediately rotate all FortiGate admin and VPN credentials, enable MFA on all management interfaces, audit for unauthorized admin accounts, and disable diagnostic sniffers; follow CISA's June 18 hardening guidance at cisa.gov.
▶ Full analysis & beginner explanation

FortiBleed is one of the most expansive credential-theft campaigns ever documented against network infrastructure. Since early 2026, the IAB combined brute-force attacks on weak passwords with reuse of credentials from prior breaches to gain administrative access to FortiGate appliances. Once inside, they deployed a custom tool called FortigateSniffer — built on top of FortiOS's own legitimate diagnostic sniffer feature — to capture in-transit credentials, tokens, secrets, and authentication hashes across 24 protocols.

As of mid-June 2026, confirmed credentials from roughly 86,644 unique devices had been catalogued, representing approximately half of all internet-facing Fortinet firewalls. The broader campaign total has grown to 430,000+ compromised devices and over 110 million stolen credentials. CISA issued an advisory on June 18 urging organizations to harden Fortinet devices, citing the active mass exploitation.

The IAB monetizes stolen access by selling it to ransomware operators, making this a critical enabler of downstream ransomware attacks across every sector. The combination of scale (194 countries, 21,600+ domains) and the method of weaponizing vendor-provided diagnostic tools makes detection and remediation especially difficult.

🎓 For beginners: A firewall is like a security guard for a company's network — FortiGate is a brand of firewall used by hundreds of thousands of businesses worldwide. Criminals found that many of these firewalls used weak or reused passwords, then turned the firewall's own built-in tools against it to silently copy every username and password flowing through the device. 👤 Who's affected: At least nine organizations have publicly confirmed impact including HackerOne, Huntress, Jamf, OneTrust, Recorded Future, Snyk, and Tanium, with roughly two dozen companies total notifying customers of the incident.
✅ What to do: Audit and revoke any third-party OAuth tokens connected to your Salesforce instance, review third-party SaaS vendors' access permissions, and enable Salesforce's Event Monitoring for anomalous API access.
▶ Full analysis & beginner explanation

The Klue breach is a textbook supply chain attack: rather than attacking HackerOne, Huntress, or Snyk directly (companies with strong security practices), the Icarus cybercrime group targeted a third-party vendor — Klue — that these firms trusted. Klue is a competitive intelligence platform used by many tech companies. After gaining access via a legacy (old) credential, attackers obtained the OAuth tokens Klue used to connect to its customers' Salesforce accounts.

OAuth tokens are essentially access keys that let one application read another's data without needing a password. By stealing these tokens, the attackers could read and download data from the Salesforce CRM systems of every Klue customer — which held sales pipeline data, customer contacts, internal intelligence reports, and other sensitive business information. Icarus posted a ransom note threatening to publish the stolen data.

The attack is particularly damaging because several of the victims are cybersecurity companies themselves, potentially exposing vulnerability research, threat intelligence data, and client security configurations. The incident underscores that even security-focused organizations can be compromised through their less-scrutinized vendors.

🎓 For beginners: Imagine a master key that opens many locks — that's what an OAuth token is: it lets one app access another without re-entering a password each time. When hackers stole Klue's master keys (tokens), they could walk into the Salesforce databases of dozens of companies as if they belonged there. This is called a supply chain attack — targeting a less-guarded third-party vendor to get at the well-protected companies behind it.
No CVE assigned — credential theft + OAuth token hijacking · Severity: HIGH — Multiple cybersecurity vendors' CRM data compromised in cascading supply chain breach.

▶ Full analysis & beginner explanation

CVE-2026-50656, named "RoguePlanet" by Morphisec researchers, is a privilege escalation flaw in Microsoft Defender — the antivirus and security platform built into every modern Windows installation. The irony is stark: the tool designed to protect systems against malware becomes the attack surface itself. Attackers with a local foothold can exploit this flaw to escalate to SYSTEM privileges — the most powerful account in Windows, capable of accessing any file, process, or configuration on the machine.

Privilege escalation vulnerabilities like RoguePlanet are particularly valuable in the attack kill chain. They're typically used as a second stage: after attackers gain initial access (via phishing, a vulnerable web app, or an IAB like KongTuke), they use flaws like this to break out of low-privilege sandboxes and gain total control over the system. At SYSTEM level, they can disable security software, dump credential hashes, and move laterally across the network.

Microsoft Defender is installed on virtually every Windows endpoint worldwide, making the potential attack surface enormous. Morphisec's disclosure gives Microsoft the opportunity to patch quickly, but the vulnerability will remain dangerous until organizations apply the update.

🎓 For beginners: Your antivirus (Microsoft Defender) is supposed to stop hackers — but this bug means the antivirus itself can be used against you. If a hacker gets into your computer at a low level (like as a regular user), they can use this flaw in Defender to become the all-powerful administrator instantly. Think of it like a security guard whose uniform comes with a master key — and someone figured out how to steal that key.

💡 Today I Learned: Supply Chain Attacks ▶
A supply chain attack targets a trusted third-party vendor — like a software platform or cloud integration — rather than attacking a company directly, exploiting the trust relationship between the vendor and its customers to gain access to many organizations at once. Today's Klue breach is a perfect example: hackers couldn't easily break into security-hardened companies like HackerOne or Huntress, so they attacked a less-defended market intelligence tool those companies trusted, then used stolen OAuth "master keys" to walk into their Salesforce databases. This is why modern security teams now scrutinize every vendor in their supply chain and regularly audit third-party application permissions — your security is only as strong as the least-secure vendor you trust.
📖 Glossary: CVE · CVSS · RCE · SSRF · Zero-Day · IAB · KEV · OAuth · MFA · SD-WAN · PoC · Webshell ▶
CVE — Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures; a standardized ID (e.g. CVE-2026-35273) assigned to each publicly known security flaw so researchers and defenders can reference it unambiguously.
CVSS — Common Vulnerability Scoring System; a 0–10 scale rating how severe a security flaw is, where 9.0–10 is Critical, 7.0–8.9 is High, 4.0–6.9 is Medium, and below 4.0 is Low.
RCE — Remote Code Execution; a class of vulnerability that lets an attacker run arbitrary code on a victim's system over a network, without physical access.
SSRF — Server-Side Request Forgery; an attack where a server is tricked into making unauthorized network requests on behalf of an attacker, potentially exposing internal systems or writing malicious files.
Zero-Day — A vulnerability that is unknown to the vendor and is being actively exploited in the wild, giving defenders "zero days" to patch before attacks occur.
IAB — Initial Access Broker; a cybercriminal specialist who breaks into corporate networks and sells that persistent access to ransomware groups or other threat actors.
KEV — Known Exploited Vulnerabilities; CISA's catalog of security flaws confirmed to be actively exploited in real attacks, with mandatory remediation deadlines for federal agencies.
OAuth — Open Authorization; an open standard that lets applications access each other's data using temporary tokens instead of sharing passwords, widely used for third-party integrations.
MFA — Multi-Factor Authentication; a login method requiring two or more verification factors (e.g., password + a code sent to your phone), making stolen passwords alone insufficient to log in.
SD-WAN — Software-Defined Wide Area Network; enterprise networking technology that uses software to intelligently route traffic across multiple locations and cloud services.
PoC — Proof of Concept; a working exploit demonstration that proves a vulnerability can be triggered, often published by researchers to pressure vendors into releasing patches quickly.
Webshell — A malicious script uploaded to a web server that provides attackers with persistent remote access and code execution through an ordinary web browser, surviving reboots and patches.
APT — Advanced Persistent Threat; a sophisticated, often nation-state-sponsored attacker who quietly maintains long-term access to a target network rather than causing immediate visible damage.
